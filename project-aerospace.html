Aerospace Component Lightweighting Initiative

Advanced topology optimization for aircraft interior components

Duration: 16 months
Role: Principal Engineer - Structures
Industry: Aerospace

Project Overview

Led a comprehensive lightweighting initiative for aircraft interior components, targeting minimum 30% weight reduction without compromising safety or increasing production costs. The project encompassed seat frames, overhead bin structures, and galley equipment for a next-generation narrow-body aircraft.

Technical Challenge

Aircraft interior components must satisfy stringent requirements:

  • FAA certification requirements (16g forward, 14g lateral crash loads)
  • Fatigue life exceeding 90,000 flight cycles
  • Flamability and smoke toxicity standards (FAR 25.853)
  • Manufacturing cost targets (no more than 10% increase)
  • Maintainability and inspection requirements
  • Minimum 30% weight reduction vs. current generation

Seat Frame Assembly - Baseline vs. Optimized

Design Approach

1. Topology Optimization

Employed advanced topology optimization using Altair OptiStruct:

  • Defined design space around existing component envelopes
  • Applied multi-load case optimization (crash, vibration, fatigue)
  • Manufacturing constraints (minimum feature size, draw direction)
  • Iterative optimization to refine organic geometries

2. Material Selection Study

Evaluated advanced materials to enable weight reduction:

  • Aluminum-lithium alloys (2198-T8, 2099-T83)
  • High-strength titanium (Ti-6Al-4V) for critical joints
  • Carbon fiber composites for non-structural panels
  • Magnesium alloys for select secondary structure

Topology Optimization Results

3. Design for Additive Manufacturing

Leveraged metal 3D printing for complex geometries:

  • Selective laser melting (SLM) for titanium components
  • Lattice structures in low-stress regions
  • Integrated features to reduce part count and assembly time
  • Optimized for powder removal and support structure minimization

Structural Analysis

Comprehensive FEA validation ensured safety compliance:

  • Linear static analysis for ultimate load conditions
  • Nonlinear analysis for crash scenarios (material and geometric nonlinearity)
  • Fatigue analysis using stress-life and strain-life methods
  • Modal analysis to avoid resonance with airframe vibrations
  • Thermal analysis for galley equipment exposure

FEA Crash Load Simulation Results

Certification and Testing

Rigorous testing program to meet FAA requirements:

  • Static Testing: Ultimate load testing to 1.5x limit load
  • Dynamic Testing: Sled testing to validate crash performance
  • Fatigue Testing: 135,000 cycles (1.5x design life)
  • Flammability: Vertical burn testing per FAR 25.853
  • Corrosion: Salt spray testing per ASTM B117

Manufacturing Partnership

Collaborated with suppliers to enable production:

  • Qualified metal AM suppliers for titanium components
  • Developed casting processes for complex aluminum parts
  • Implemented automated fiber placement for composite panels
  • Established quality procedures and inspection criteria

Results and Impact

  • Weight Savings: 42% reduction exceeded 30% target (385 kg total aircraft)
  • Safety: All components maintained 2.0 safety factor under all load cases
  • Fuel Savings: $1.2M lifetime fuel cost savings per aircraft
  • Emissions: 2,400 tons CO₂ reduction over aircraft lifetime
  • Certification: FAA certification achieved on schedule
  • Cost: 8% manufacturing cost increase (within 10% target)

Innovation Highlights

  • First application of titanium lattice structures in commercial aircraft interiors
  • Novel aluminum-lithium forming processes for complex geometries
  • Integrated multi-material design reducing part count by 25%
  • Automated design optimization workflow reducing development time by 40%

Key Learnings

  • Topology optimization must be constrained by manufacturing realities from the start
  • Early supplier engagement is critical for novel manufacturing processes
  • Certification testing should inform design iterations, not just validate final design
  • Multi-material solutions can outperform single-material optimization
  • Additive manufacturing enables geometries impossible with traditional methods
