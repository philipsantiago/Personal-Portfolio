Lightweight Automotive Suspension Component

Industry: Automotive Duration: 6 months Role: Lead Design Engineer

Project Overview

This project focused on redesigning a critical lower control arm component for a major automotive manufacturer's new vehicle platform. The primary objective was to achieve significant weight reduction while maintaining or improving structural performance, fatigue life, and manufacturability.

Technical Challenge

The existing control arm design, while robust, was over-engineered in certain areas and represented a significant portion of the vehicle's unsprung mass. The challenge was to:

  • Reduce component weight by at least 25% without compromising safety factors
  • Maintain compliance with automotive safety standards (FMVSS)
  • Ensure compatibility with existing suspension geometry and mounting points
  • Achieve cost neutrality or improvement despite using advanced materials
  • Design for high-volume manufacturing processes

Approach & Methodology

I employed a comprehensive engineering approach combining multiple analysis techniques:

1. Initial Analysis

  • Conducted finite element analysis (FEA) on existing design to identify stress concentrations
  • Performed multi-body dynamics simulation to capture real-world loading scenarios
  • Analyzed failure modes from field data and durability testing

2. Design Optimization

  • Utilized topology optimization in ANSYS to identify optimal material distribution
  • Evaluated alternative materials including high-strength aluminum alloys and advanced composites
  • Created parametric CAD models in SolidWorks for rapid design iteration
  • Applied design for manufacturing (DFM) principles for forging and machining operations

3. Validation

  • Performed comprehensive FEA including static, fatigue, and impact loading scenarios
  • Conducted physical prototyping using CNC machining
  • Executed laboratory durability testing to validate fatigue life predictions
  • Collaborated with manufacturing team for producibility assessment

Key Learnings

This project reinforced the importance of balancing multiple competing objectives in engineering design. While topology optimization provided excellent starting points, significant engineering judgment was required to translate organic shapes into manufacturable geometries. The collaboration between design, analysis, and manufacturing teams was critical to achieving both technical and business objectives.

Additionally, early engagement with suppliers regarding material selection and processing capabilities proved invaluable in ensuring the final design could be produced at scale without quality compromises.
